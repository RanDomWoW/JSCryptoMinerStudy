<h1>
Twine is an open-source tool for telling interactive, nonlinear stories.
</h1>

<p>
You don't need to write any code to create a simple story with Twine, but you
can extend your stories with variables, conditional logic, images, CSS, and
JavaScript when you're ready.
</p>

<p>
Twine publishes directly to HTML, so you can post your work nearly anywhere.
Anything you create with it is completely free to use any way you like, including
for commercial purposes.
</p>

<p>
Twine was originally created by <a href="https://chrisklimas.com/">Chris
Klimas</a> in 2009 and is now maintained by a whole bunch of people at <a
href="https://github.com/tweecode">several</a> <a
href="https://bitbucket.org/_L_/harlowe">different</a> <a
href="https://github.com/klembot/twinejs">repositories</a>.
</p>

<section id="quotes" class="paper bluepin">

<p>
<strong>A new tool has emerged that empowers just about anyone to create a game.</strong>
It's called Twine. It's extremely easy to use, and it has already given rise to a
lively and diverse development scene. 
</p>

<p>
<cite class="smaller"><a href="http://www.gamespot.com/articles/power-to-the-people-the-text-adventures-of-twine/1100-6402665/">Carolyn Petit</a>, Gamespot</cite>
</p>

<p>
Although plenty of independent games venture where mainstream games fear to
tread, <b>Twine represents something even more radical:</b> the transformation of
video games into something that is not only consumed by the masses but also
created by them.
</p>

<p>
<cite class="smaller"><a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/11/23/magazine/twine-the-video-game-technology-for-all.html">Laura Hudson</a>, The New York Times Magazine</cite>
</p>

<p>
<strong>The simple beauty of Twine is this:</strong> if you can type words and occasionally put
brackets around some of those words, you can make a Twine game.
</p>

<p>
<cite class="smaller">
<a href="http://gamejolt.com/articles/twine-tips-for-beginners/7347/">Kitty Horrorshow</a>
</cite>
</p>

<p>
<strong>If you're interested in making interactive fiction then there's no better place
to start than Twine.</strong> It's possibly the simplest game making tool available, it
will take you mere minutes to get started, and it has a wonderfully simple
visual editor.
</p>

<p>
<cite class="smaller">
<a href="http://youcanmakevideogames.com/tools/twine">Richard Perrin</a>
</cite>
</p>

<p>
And aside from being free, it's really not programming at all &mdash;
<b>if you can write a story, you can make a Twine game.</b>
</p>

<p>
<cite class="smaller">
<a href="http://www.theguardian.com/technology/gamesblog/2013/apr/10/anna-anthropy-twine-revolution">Anna Anthropy</a>
</cite>
</p>

<p>
<b>Twine is the closest we've come to a blank page.</b> It binds itself and it
can bind itself along an infinite number of spines extending in any direction.
</p>

<p>
<cite class="smaller">
<a href="http://nightmaremode.thegamerstrust.com/2012/11/25/creation-under-capitalism/">Porpentine</a>
</cite>
</p>
