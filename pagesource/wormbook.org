      <div class="box">
	<strong>WormBook</strong> is a comprehensive, open-access collection of original,
	peer-reviewed chapters covering topics related to the biology of <em>Caenorhabditis
          elegans</em> and other nematodes. <strong>Wormbook</strong> also contains: <strong>WormMethods</strong>, a collection of
	protocols for nematode researchers; <strong>WormHistory</strong>, personal perspectives on <em>C. elegans</em>
	research; and the <strong>Worm Breeder's Gazette</strong>, an informal, non-refereed, biannual
	newsletter for the interchange of ideas and information related to <em>C. elegans</em> and
	other nematodes. Additional chapters can be found in GENETICS.

	<div class="item">
	  <div class="date">January 30, 2018</div>
	  <div class="title">Tubulin chapter added to WormBook</div>
	  <div class="entry">
<a href="/chapters/www_tubulins/tubulins_ePub.pdf"  target="_blank">Tubulins in <em>C. elegans</em></a>, by Daryl Hurd, has just been pre-published ahead of formal publication in the Cell Biology section.  This chapter describes the large family of tubulins in <em>C. elegans</em>, their cellular and developmental roles, biogenesis, and post-translational modifications.
	</div>
	</div>

	<div class="item">
	  <div class="date">March 23 2017</div>
	  <div class="title">History Chapter added to WormBook</div>
	  <div class="entry">
<a href="/chapters/www_nematodeshistory/nematodeshistory_ePub.pdf"  target="_blank">History of research on <em>C. elegans</em> and other free-living nematodes as model organisms</a>, by Victor Marc Nigon and Marie-Anne F&eacute;lix, has been added to the History section of Wormbook as a pre-publication.  This chapter reviews the early history of using free-living nematodes as models to address general questions in biology. It focuses on the period beginning in 1899 with the first publication of Emile Maupas mentioning <em>Rhabditis elegans</em> and ending in 1974 with the first publications by Sydney Brenner.
	</div>
	</div>

	<div class="item">
	  <div class="date">August 23 2016</div>
	  <div class="title">New chapter added to Cell Biology</div>
	  <div class="entry">
<a href="/chapters/www_bodywallmuscle/bodywallmuscle.pdf"  target="_blank">Development, structure, and maintenance of <em>C. elegans</em> body wall muscle</a>, by Kathrin Gieseler, Hiroshi Qadota, and Guy Benian, has been pre-published in the Cell Biology section. This chapter partially updates and greatly expands on <a href="/chapters/www_SarcomereAssembCelegansmusc/SarcomereAssembCelegansmusc.html"  target="_blank">Sarcomere assembly in <em>C. elegans</em> muscle<a> by Don Moerman and Ben Williams. The new chapter includes detailed descriptions of sarcomere components, their assembly, organization, regulation, and function. It also includes a discussion of insights into dystrophin function from <em>C. elegans</em> studies.
	</div>
	</div>

	<div class="item">
	  <div class="date">July 14 2016</div>
	  <div class="title">New chapter added to WormMethods</div>
	  <div class="entry">
<a href="/chapters/www_dauerlarvae/dauerlarvae.pdf"  target="_blank">Working with dauer larvae</a>, by Xantha Karp, has been added to the Behavior section of WormMethods as an ePub ahead of formal publication. This chapter summarizes dauer formation and describes methods for inducing and isolating  dauers. Methods for obtaining  pre- and post-dauer larvae are also provided.
	</div>
	</div>

	<div class="item">
	  <div class="date">May 24 2016</div>
	  <div class="title">Small GTPase chapter updated</div>
	  <div class="entry">
<a href="/chapters/www_smallGTPases.2/smallGTPases.2.pdf"  target="_blank">Small GTPases (2016)</a>, by David Reiner and Erik Lundquist, has been added to the Signal transduction section. This chapter updates the <a href="/chapters/www_smallGTPases/smallGTPases.html"  target="_blank">2006 chapter</a> by Lundquist with the same title. The new chapter provides an overview of small GTPases in <em>C. elegans</em> and gives details for members of the Ras, Rho, Rab, Arf, and Ran families. Atypical nematode members that fall outside these subfamilies are also discussed.
	</div>
