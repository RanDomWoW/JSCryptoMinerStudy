	  <h3 class="catItemTitle">
			
	  				<a href="/news/concerns-about-canadian-international-trade-tribunal-decision-on-centrifugal-opening-knives">
	  		Concerns About Canadian International Trade Tribunal Decision on Centrifugal Opening Knives	  	</a>
	  	
	  		  </h3>
	  
				<!-- Item Author -->
		<span class="catItemAuthor">
			Written by						<a rel="author" href="http://multitool.org/">Multitool.org Team </a>
					</span>

	  <div class="catItemIntroText">
	  	<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><img src="/cache/klixok2watermark/a74f7052922645509baf497a24991bd6.jpg" alt="Yellow Spyderco" width="400" height="300" style="margin-right: 5px; margin-bottom: 5px; float: left;" />Here at <a href="http://www.multitool.org" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Multitool.org</a> we pay close attention to industry trends and changes to legislation that could impact our Members. As always, we promote the safe and enjoyable use of multitools, fixed blades, folding knives, one piece multitools and more.</span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><b>A Canadian love affair with folding knives.</b></span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;">Many of our Members are campers, hikers and general outdoorspeople. We use folding knives as tools for our everyday life - as useful extensions of ourselves. Folders (folding knives) are extremely popular for a multitude of reasons, <em>especially</em> safety and convenience. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;">Although extremely popular, fixed blades have been known to pop through a leather sheath or two, but a folder tucks neatly inside itself for safety's sake. Their modest size makes them the perfect carrying knife for cutting up apples on a hike, to cutting a piece of rope while setting up camp. </span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;">One handed opening folding knives are extremely convenient for the busy Canadian camper. Cold, wet hands can making opening up a traditional Swiss Army Knife quite difficult. Whereas one-handed opening blades like the Victorinox one-handed Trekker are simple and efficient. </span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;">This week, the Canadian International Trade Tribunal (CITT) rendered a decision in<a href="http://www.citt-tcce.gc.ca/en/node/8176" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"> Appeal No. AP-2017-012, T. LaPlante v. President of the Canada Border Services Agency</a> regarding knives which open automatically by centrifugal force (centrifugal knives).</span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="font-size: 12pt; font-family: helvetica;"><span style="color: #000000;"><span style="color: #000000;"><b>Why this decision matters.</b></span></span></span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><a href="https://www.cbsa-asfc.gc.ca/publications/cn-ad/cn18-01-eng.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">CBSA has modified their prohibited weapons classifications</a> to include a very large selection of folding knives. And they're calling them 'folding knives' by name! Not just 'centrifugal or gravity knives'. That means, that this large selection could no longer be legally imported into Canada. <span style="text-decoration: underline;">It's also worth speculating that they could become prohibited weapons by law in the future.</span> This is setting precedent. </span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><img src="https://media.giphy.com/media/3ohc0WhpJCOtFpK0jC/giphy.gif" alt="MTO Gif opening knife" style="margin-top: 5px; margin-bottom: 5px; margin-left: 5px; float: right;" />The new definition states that centrifugal knives will be classified as prohibited weapons if the following conditions are met: </span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><em><span style="color: #000000;">A) a knife has a blade that opens by centrifugal force, when the blade is released from the handle into the fully ejected and locked position with a simple and brisk outwardly flick of the wrist; and</span></em></span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><em><span style="color: #000000;">B) it includes knives that require some preliminary or simultaneous minimal manipulation of either a flipper or other non-edged parts of the blade.</span></em></span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><em><span style="color: #000000;"></span></em><span style="color: #000000;"><b>But what does it really mean for folding knives in Canada?</b></span></span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;">When you look at point 'A' it doesn't cause much of a concern. But &ldquo;B&rdquo; changes <em>everything.</em> A non-edged part of the blade could include an opening thumb hole or a stud. It could mean that you gently released the blade yourself with your thumb along the side of the blade. Once released, many if not most folding knives will open easily or after 2-3 tries with a 'brisk outwardly flick of the wrist'. The full CITT decision allows for multiple attempts. We are extremely concerned that the majority of folding knives in Canada will be classified as prohibited weapons as this new definition lacks specificity.</span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><b>We tested the CBSA definition on common folding knives &ndash; the results weren't good.</b></span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;">We went through our vast collection and tried out many folding knives found at Canadian Tire, Cabela's and other popular stores. Of course, some folding knives were easier to open than others. But once you add the definition of knives 'that require some preliminary or simultaneous minimal manipulation of either a flipper or other non-edged parts of the blade' - almost all of the folding knives we tested failed the test. </span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;">For example, the popular Swiss Army Knife, the <a href="http://sakwiki.com/tiki-index.php?page=Trekker" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Victorinox Trekker</a> or the <a href="http://sakwiki.com/tiki-index.php?page=Forester+Victorinox" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Forrester M Grip</a> (seen in the gif) failed the test and would be classified as prohibited based on the CBSA definition. With a very slight manipulation of the thumb hole, and with a 'brisk outwardly flick of the wrist' the knife blade locks into place. We also tested the Pictou, NS Canadian-made Grohman R300S Lockblade &ndash; it failed the test and would be classified as prohibited as well. </span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><b>Multitool.org's stance on the CITT decision and CBSA notice regarding Centrifugal Opening Knives</b></span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;">We believe that the CITT decision and CBSA notice do not reflect the Canadian government's intention or the spirit of the law. </span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;">The Criminal Code of Canada classifies a prohibited weapon (knife) as ' a blade that opens automatically by gravity or centrifugal force or by hand pressure applied to a button, spring or other device in or attached to the handle of the knife'. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;">And we firmly believe that the spirit of this law is to protect Canadians, especially law-enforcement. We do not believe that Canada's Parliamentarians would call for a ban on basic folding knives which are so popular from coast to coast to coast. </span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><b><span style="color: #000000;">What can we do?</span></b></span></p>
<p class="western" style="margin-bottom: 0cm;"><span style="font-family: helvetica; font-size: 12pt;"><span style="color: #000000;">Continue to be law-abiding users of the tools you own. If you're in Canada,<a href="http://www.ourcommons.ca/parliamentarians/en/constituencies/FindMP" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"> reach out to your Member of Parliament </a>and let them know your concerns. Let them know that you believe the CBSA notice does not reflect the spirit of the </span><span style="color: #000000;"><i>Criminal Code of Canada.&nbsp;</i></span></span></p>	  </div>

	  <div class="catItemIntroText">
	  	<p>According to our friends over at Knifenews.com (full story <a href="http://knifenews.com/breaking-smith-wesson-to-acquire-taylor-brands-and-launch-new-bti-tools-sub-division/?utm_source=KnifeNews+List&amp;utm_campaign=81a3bb3120-smith_wesson7_18_2016&amp;utm_medium=email&amp;utm_term=0_575065eac1-81a3bb3120-121672917" target="_blank">HERE</a>) the famous firearms manufacturer Smith &amp; Wesson has purchased Taylor Brands LLC for $85 million.&nbsp; For those who don't know, Taylor has long produced knives and multitools with the Smith &amp; Wesson name on them, and I have often commented on the quality (more accurately, the lack therof) of Taylor knives, whether they are marked S&amp;W, Schrade, Uncle Henry or Imperial.&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>For years I had labored under the impression that the folks at S&amp;W were blissfully unaware of the crap that Taylor was producing with their name on it- I am a big fan of Smith &amp; Wesson firearms, and that name has become (in my mind at least) synonomous with quality revolvers.&nbsp; I had assumed that someone at Taylor had arranged for a dump truck full of cash to arrive on a bi-monthly schedule at the house of S&amp;W's licensing manager, and that as a result, he or she just didn't ask any questions.&nbsp; It seems that isn't the case, and that S&amp;W is fully aware of the crap that Taylor is producing, and that is very disapointing to me.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The worst part is that S&amp;W doesn't appear to even want to fix it- they seem content to merely take advantage of the distribution network that Taylor has, so that they can make their own in house accessories more available.&nbsp; As if having their name on crappy knives wasn't damaging enough to the brand, now Smith &amp; Wesson will be selling their actual merchandise on the shelves right next to crud, strengthening the connection.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>This is one of those times when I really hope I am wrong.&nbsp; As I said, I have lots of respect for S&amp;W, and I hate to see them take a bad decision (like giving a license to Taylor in the first place) and make it worse.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
