<a name="more"></a><h3>&quot;Phrases in English&quot; (PIE) and the British National Corpus</h3>

<p>The <a href="http://www.natcorp.ox.ac.uk/" target="_blank">British National Corpus</a>
(BNC) is a carefully-selected collection of
4124 contemporary written and spoken English texts, primarily from the United
Kingdom.&nbsp; The
corpus totals over 100 million words and
<a href="http://www.natcorp.ox.ac.uk/corpus/creating.xml">covers a representative range of domains,
genres and registers</a>. The entire corpus has been analyzed and marked up
with <a href="POScodes.html">part of speech</a> (PoS) tags. Provenance and
other attributes are carefully documented for each text. "<a target="_blank" href="http://www.natcorp.ox.ac.uk/corpus/">What
is the BNC</a>?" provides a succinct  overview of the corpus;
<i> &nbsp;</i>for an exhaustive description, consult the
<i> <a target="_blank" href="http://www.natcorp.ox.ac.uk/XMLedition/URG/">British National Corpus Users Reference Guide</a></i>.&nbsp;
<a href="http://web.archive.org/web/20060118223001/http:/applicata.clifo.unibo.it/Archivio/1998-99/aprile/BNC+handbook.html">Chapter 1</a> of Guy
Aston and Lou Burnard's <i>BNC Handbook</i> includes an informative survey of possible uses of corpora
in general and of the BNC in particular.&nbsp; Additional useful information and resources (including
various frequency lists with more refined PoS tagging) are found on the
<a href="http://ucrel.lancs.ac.uk/bncfreq/">companion website</a> for
<i><a target="_blank" href="http://ucrel.lancs.ac.uk/bncfreq/">Word Frequencies in Written and Spoken English based on the British National
Corpus</a></i> by Geoffrey Leech, Paul Rayson and Andrew Wilson.&nbsp; The
introduction includes a very readable discussion of how the corpus was tokenized
and tagged.</p>


<p>PIE incorporates a database derived from the second or World Edition of
the BNC (2000), but is not affiliated with the BNC Consortium. It aims to provide a simple yet powerful interface for studying words
and phrases up to eight words long appropriate for both experienced
researchers and novice users.&nbsp; For investigating words in longer contexts,
the full BNC corpus and Xaira search and analysis software is available on CD-ROM from the <a href="http://www.natcorp.ox.ac.uk/" target="_blank">
BNC Consortium</a> (a single user license costs only £ 75).&nbsp; Alternatively,
one can
<a href="http://sara.natcorp.ox.ac.uk/lookup.html">look up individual words and
phrases online</a>.</p>


<p>To understand and interpret the datasets produced here and to compare them to
results of direct queries to BNC, please <a href="howmade.html">read how and why
the original data were normalized to build the PIE database</a>.</p>


<h3>What can PIE do?</h3>

<p>Explore the distribution of words and phrases in English via various query
interfaces: </p>
<ul>
	<li><a target="_blank" href="explore.html"><i>N-grams</i></a><a> are sequences of
	<i>n</i> words, where
<i>n</i> falls in the range 1-8, and <i>word</i> means a token of any lexical
entity assigned a PoS tag by the CLAWS parser (<a href="POScodes.html">details</a>). For example, the most frequent 1-gram in the BNC data is
	<i>the, </i>and<i> the end
of the</i> tops the list of 4-grams. </li>
	<li><i><a target="_blank" href="explorep.html">Phrase-frames</a></i> are <b>sets of variants</b> of an n-gram identical
except for one word, represented here by the wildcard symbol *. The most frequent (and
most productive,
<i>i.e.</i> having the greatest number of variants) 4-frame is<i> the * of
the,</i> with 5652 variants such as <i>the end of the, the rest of the, the top
of the, the nature of the</i> etc.&nbsp; </li>
	<li><i><a target="_blank" href="explorepg.html">PoS-grams</a></i> are patterns
of <b>P</b>art <b>o</b>f <b>S</b>peech tags assigned to word forms without
reference to the specific lexical entities.&nbsp; When ordered by <b>types,</b>
the most frequent &quot;3-PoS-gram&quot; is ART ADJ NOUN, e.g. <i>the other hand.</i>&nbsp;
On the other hand, when ordered by <b>tokens,</b> the 3-PoS-gram PREP ART NOUN
as in <i>at the end</i> are more frequent.&nbsp; </li>
	<li><i><a target="_blank" href="explorec.html">Char-grams</a></i> are
sequences of <i>n</i> letters.&nbsp; Their distribution can be studied by
	position (initial, medial, final) as well as by frequency in tokens or
	types.&nbsp; Unsurprisingly, <i>the</i> is the most frequent 3-char-gram by
	tokens (8,222,751 tokens, 1007 types), but <i>ing</i> has the most distinct
	types (2,991,683 tokens, 9416 types).</li>
</ul>


<p>Each query returns datasets in &quot;chunks&quot; of up to 100,000
items, and queries can be repeated until all matching data have
been retrieved.&nbsp; Results can be ordered alphabetically, by frequency or by
PoS tag. For
focused studies, users can &quot;filter&quot; results for specific word-forms and / or word-classes
which a query must match or exclude.&nbsp; All query interfaces offer full support for wildcards.&nbsp;
A click on any phrase brings up example concordances from the BNC.&nbsp;
Details are found in
the <a href="tutorials.html">tutorials</a>. </p>

<p>This site also supports
querying with regular expressions and downloading an entire dataset matching a query in
tabbed format for import into a database.  Ultimately tools developed for
<a target="_blank" href="http://www.kwicfinder.com/">KWiCFinder</a> and
<a target="_blank" href="http://www.kwicfinder.com/kfNgram/">kfNgram</a> will
permit browsing and analysis of the datasets via a graphical user interface on
the PC. Slight modifications to <a href="howmade.html">data normalization conventions</a>
may result in minor discrepancies in frequencies reported for the two versions
of the database.

<p>As site developer I also gratefully acknowledge my debt to Michael Stubbs of the
University of Trier for fruitful e-mail discussions that led to the creation and
refinement of
this database and Web site. It was Stubbs who generously suggested that I add
support for &quot;phrase-frames&quot; to
<a target="_blank" href="http://www.kwicfinder.com/kfNgram/">kfNgram</a>.
This concept originated with his research assistant Isabel Barth, who also
implemented the original phrase-frame generator. Their collaboration led to the
insightful paper &quot;Using recurrent phrases as text-type discriminators: a
quantitative method and some findings&quot; <i>(Functions of Language </i>(10, 1, 2003).&nbsp;
<a target="_blank" href="http://www.kwicfinder.com/kfNgram/">kfNgram</a> was
originally developed for a
<a target="_blank" href="http://www.kwicfinder.com/AAACL2002whf.pdf">comparative study of a
corpus I compiled from the Web with
data from the BNC</a>. When I remarked that generating lists of <i>all</i> the
n-grams and phrase-frames in the BNC would really test the limits of
<a target="_blank" href="http://www.kwicfinder.com/kfNgram/kfNgramHelp.html">kfNgram</a>, Stubbs
encouraged me to do it and suggested breaking the lists down further by
domain and genre. The goal has evolved from a collection of overwhelmingly
large static lists into  databases which produce manageable datasets tailored
to the user's research needs. Four of Stubbs' works available online survey and
illustrate core concepts and point the way to exploring words and phrases:
