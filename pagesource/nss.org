<p class="HP_News_Title">SpaceX&rsquo;s Falcon Heavy Test Flight Brings National Space Society&rsquo;s Vision of a Return to the Moon and a Spacefaring Civilization Closer</p>
<p class="HP_News_Text">(Washington, DC -- February 7, 2018)  &nbsp;NSS congratulates SpaceX on the first flight of the Falcon Heavy(FH). FH opens an era of lower launch costs that will enable a wide range of new endeavors in space, including an affordable return to the Moon. &ldquo;The FH will enable concept studies like the <a href="http://www.nss.org/docs/EvolvableLunarArchitecture.pdf" target="_blank">Evolvable Lunar Architecture</a> to become a reality, allowing the U.S.A. to return to the Moon within the current NASA budget while maintaining a balanced space program, including a gapless transition to future low Earth orbit commercial space stations and robotic exploration of the solar system,&rdquo; said NSS Senior Vice President Bruce Pittman. &ldquo;NSS members look forward to seeing NASA join the U.S. military in making use of the commercially competitive FH, now the most capable rocket currently flying.&rdquo; <a href="http://blog.nss.org/spacexs-falcon-heavy-test-flight-brings-national-space-societys-vision-of-a-return-to-the-moon-and-a-spacefaring-civilization-closer/" target="_blank">See full press release</a>.</p>

<p class="HP_News_Title">High School Students Take on Challenge to Design World&rsquo;s First Science-Fiction Inspired Spacecraft</p>
<p class="HP_News_Text">(Washington, DC -- June 28, 2017)&nbsp;  SpaceWorks  Enterprises, Inc. hosted a summer program specifically geared for high  school students, challenging them to design the <i>NSS Enterprise</i> spacecraft envisioned by Enterprise In Space  (EIS), a non-profit program of the National Space Society (NSS). The  students had access to numerous resources, including the expertise of  SpaceWorks and EIS staff. The  SpaceWorks program is called ASTRO, or Aerospace Summer Training &amp;  Research Opportunity, a project-oriented experience  during which participants work in teams to solve an aerospace  engineering design problem. <p class="HP_News_Title">New in the NSS Space Settlement Journal: Orbital Space Settlement Radiation Shielding</p>
<p class="HP_News_Text">(Washington, DC -- April 29, 2017)  &nbsp;<a href="http://www.nss.org/settlement/journal/NSS-JOURNAL-Orbital-Space-Settlement-Radiation-Shielding.pdf" target="_blank">Orbital Space Settlement Radiation Shielding</a>, a paper by Al Globus and Joe Strout, has just been published in the&nbsp;<a href="http://www.nss.org/settlement/journal/index.html" target="_blank">NSS Space Settlement Journal</a>. The authors examine the radiation shielding requirements for protecting the inhabitants of orbital space settlements.  In a surprising result, radiation measurements on the International Space Station (ISS) and our calculations using OLTARIS, NASA&rsquo;s online radiation computational tool, indicate that space settlements in Equatorial Low Earth Orbit (ELEO) below about 500 km are likely to meet safety standards with little or no dedicated radiation shielding. This reduces the mass of typical orbital space settlement designs by 95% or more, suggesting that the easiest place to build the first space settlements is in ELEO due to proximity to Earth and relatively low system mass.</p>

<p class="HP_News_Text"><strong>Abstract.</strong> The geomagnetic field seems to be collapsing.&nbsp; This has happened many times in the deep past, but never since civilization began.&nbsp; One implication is that the cost of space settlement will increase substantially if we do not expedite deployment of initial facilities in low Earth orbit.&nbsp; Another implication, less certain but much more damaging, is that the collapse may lead to catastrophic global cooling before the end of this century.&nbsp; We must establish self-sufficient communities off Earth before that happens.&nbsp; <a href="http://www.nss.org/settlement/journal/NSS-JOURNAL-Losing-the-Geomagnetic-Shield.pdf" target="pdf">Read full paper</a>.</p>
