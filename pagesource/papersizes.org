<div class = "title"><h1>International Paper Sizes & Formats</h1></div>

<a href="http://www.iso.org/iso/catalogue_detail.htm?csnumber=36631">ISO 216 Standard</a>, 
and to provide size charts to allow a quick lookup of sizes.</p>

<h2>ISO Paper Sizes Standard Overview & Rationale</h2>

<p>The ISO paper size A standard is based on each size being half of the size of the 
previous one, when folded parallel to the shorter lengths. This system allows for a variety
of useful applications, such as the enlarging and reducing of images without any cutoff 
or margins, or folding to make a booklet of the next size down.</p>
<p>The mathematics behind this useful feature is that the sheets have an aspect ratio
(that's the ratio of the length to the width) of the square root of 2.</p>

<div id="imrt1">
<a href="/a-series-full-sized-diagram.htm"><img src="/images/a-series-paper-sizes-2.jpg" class="imrt" alt="A Series Paper Size Diagram" /></a>
<p class="crdt">Image courtesy of <a href="http://www.office365.co.uk">Office 365</a>.</p>

<h3>A Series Paper Sizes Formal Definition</h3>

<p>The ISO 216 definiton of the A paper sizes is based on the following basis:</p>
<ul>
<li>The length divided by the width is 1.4142</li>
<li>The A0 size has an area of 1 square metre.</li>
<li>Each subsequent size A(n) is defined as A(n-1) cut in half parallel to its shorter sides.</li>
<li>The standard length and width of each size is rounded to the nearest millimetre.</li>
</ul>
<p>Note: The last item is there because the root 2 aspect ratio doesn't always give a
whole number.</p>

<p><a href="/a-paper-sizes.htm">Sizes for A Series Paper - A0, A1, A2, A3, A4, A5, A6, A7 and A8</a></p>

<h3>Extensions - B Series Paper Sizes</h3>

<p>There are some requirements for paper sizes where the A series isn't suitable and to 
take these into account the B series paper sizes were introduced. In order to explain the
rationale behind the B paper sizes, we are going to need a bit more maths.</p>

<p>The B series paper standards are also based around the 1:root 2 aspect ratio, but
in order to provide sizes not covered by the A series, the length and width of size B(n) 
are defined as being the geometric mean of size A(n) and size A(n-1).</p>

<p>Note: The geometric mean of two numbers is the square root of the product of those 
two numbers. e.g. The geometric mean of 6 and 4 is root(6x4) or the square root of 24. 
(For those who are interested - this link explains <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_mean">Geometric 
Means</a> in more detail.)</p>

<p>The benefit of using the geometric mean is that the magnification factor between
A1 and B1 sizes is that same as the one that scales B1 to A0.</p>

<p><a href="/b-paper-sizes.htm">Sizes for B Series Paper - B0, B1, B2, B3, B4, B5, B6, B7 and B8</a></p>

<h3>The C Series Envelope Sizes</h3>

<p>The C series sizes were introduced to define the sizes of envelopes suitable for the 
A series paper sizes. This is also based on the root 2 aspect ratio and the size of
a C(n) envelope is defined as the geometric mean of paper sizes A(n) and B(n). This leads to a 
C(n) envelope which nicely holds a sheet of A(n) paper unfolded.</p>

<p>This sizing also has some neat properties when dealing with folded paper, so a 
C4 envelope will hold a sheet of A4 paper unfolded, a C5 envelope will hold a sheet
of A4 paper folded in half once parallel to its shortest sides and a C6 envelope
will hold the same piece of paper folded twice.</p>

<p><a href="/c-envelope-sizes.htm">Sizes for C Series Envelopes - C0, C1, C2, C3, C4, C5, C6, C7 and C8</a></p>

<h3>RA &amp; SRA Untrimmed Paper Sizes</h3>
<p>Untrimmed raw paper formats for commercial printing are covered by ISO 217 which defines RA
and SRA paper paper sizes. These sizes are slightly larger than the corresponding A sizes to 
allow for ink bleed during the printing process. Full <a href="/ra-sra-untrimmed.htm">RA & SRA definitions here</a>.</p>

<h3>Conversions Between Paper Sizes</h3>
<p>The ISO 216 paper sizes have some useful properties for reducing and enlarging in print or photocopying
such that the scale factor needed to convert from A3 to A4, at 71% is the same as the scale factor to 
convert between A4 and A5, A5 and A6 and so on. The following link covers <a href="/magnification-scale.htm">Full 
Tables of Magnification Scale Factors for A &amp; B Paper Sizes</a>.</p>

<h3>Paper Size Areas</h3>
<p>The A series paper sizes are defined by the area of A0 being 1 square metre. The areas of 
other sizes are then: area of An is 1 divided by two to the power of n. More 
information on <a href="/a-paper-size-areas.htm">areas of standard paper sizes here</a>.</p>

<h2>American Paper Sizes</h2>
<p>The United States use their own standard paper sizes, Letter, Legal and Ledger/Tabloid
being some of the most common. There is an ANSI standard with sizes A-E and architectural 
paper sizes, the Arch series again with sizes A-E. Both of these series have been extended
for expediency with the additions of ANSI B+ and Arch E1. See 
<a href="/us-paper-sizes.htm">American Paper Sizes</a> for more information.</p>

<h2>Poster &amp; Billboard Sizes</h2>
<p>Billboards and posters use a variety of sizes some of which are in the standard
paper sizes (ISO, ANSI and Arch) others of which are specific to the poster and 
billboard forms, this is expecially true of movie posters. Click the following
links for <a href="/poster-sizes.htm">Poster</a> and <a href="/billboard-sizes.htm">Billboard</a> sizes.</p>

<p>Not all paper is the same shade of white and <a href="/whiteness-brightness.htm">Whiteness, Brightness and Shade</a>
are different measures used to discriminate between various shades of paper.</p>

<h2>Portrait & Landscape Paper</h2>

<p>Paper can be viewed as <a href="portrait-landscape.htm">Portrait paper or Landscape paper</a> depending on
how you look at it. Viewed with the shorter sides at the top and bottom it is said to be Portrait and viewed
with the longer sides at the top and bottom it is said to be Landscape.</p>

<h2>Historical Paper Sizes</h2>

<p>Before the introduction of ISO 216 many paper sizes were in use around the world. <a href="old-imperial-sizes.htm">British 
Imperial paper sizes</a> were common in countries which had been part of the British Empire, Japan had traditional 
paper sizes and a variety of sizes were used across European countries.</p>
