<div class="inner_container"><h2>What Does RN Stand For?</h2>
<p>RN stands for Registered Nurse.<br />
<b> </b></p>
<h2>What Is a Registered Nurse (RN)?</h2>
<p>A Registered Nurse is licensed by the State for provide medical care to patients in various settings. He or she is extensively trained in critical thinking, anatomy and physiology, biology, chemistry, pharmacology, and courses specific to nursing, which provides the framework needed to accurately assess and intervene for sick patients.</p>
<p>RNs are licensed by each state after successfully completing a RN training program and passing the National Council Licensure Examination, or NCLEX-RN.</p>
<p>The minimum degree required is an Associate's Degree in Nursing, usually a 2-year program. Prerequisites for acceptance may be required.<br />
<b> </b></p>
<h2>What Are RN Duties?</h2>
<p>Duties vary for RNs depending on where they work.</p>
<p>In general, RNs are responsible for complex head-to-toe assessments, developing a plan of care, carrying out physician orders, starting and maintaining invasive lines and devices, and safe medication administration. Learn more about <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/guide/working-rn/#rns">what an RN does</a> and <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/guide/bsn-nurse-roles/">nursing roles</a>.<br />
<b> </b></p>
<h2>Where Do RN's Work?</h2>
<p>RNs can work in many, many different settings.</p>
<p>In just about every area of a hospital, clinics, physician's offices, working from home for an insurance company, for themselves as a legal nurse consultant, as an emergency responder, as a traveling nurse, as an internationally traveling nurse, as an instructor, and many more.</p>
<p>The job opportunities for RNs are practically endless and they just keep growing!</p>
<p>Read more about <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/guide/working-rn/#rns-work">where RN's work</a>, <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/rn-salary/">how much an RN makes</a>, <a href="https://jobs.registerednursing.org/">find RN jobs</a>, and <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/aprn/">APRN careers</a>.</p>
<h2>How Do I Become an RN?<b><br />
</b></h2>
<p>The minimum degree to become a RN is an Associate's Degree in Nursing, usually a 2-year degree. Prerequisites vary by school and are sometimes required before acceptance into the nursing program.</p>
<p>Once the RN has an Associate's and is licensed, he or she can attend a RN to BSN program either online or in-person. The Bachelor's of Science in Nursing, or BSN, is not required by every place of employment but some do require it. Usually the RN is hired with an Associate's degree and the hospital has a set time frame for BSN completion. Often they will <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/guide/cost/#ways-pay-rn-school">help the RN pay for school</a>.</p>
<p>Learn more about <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/how-to-become/">how to become an RN</a>, the <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/teas/">TEAS test</a>, and <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/entry-level-nursing/">entry level nursing</a> pathways.</p>
<h2>How Do I Find an RN Program That's Right for Me?</h2>
<p>Finding the right RN program can be challenging. The best ways to find a good fit are to research local schools, tour them, and compare requirements, cost, and length of program.</p>
<p>Consider NCLEX-RN "pass rates" when thinking about the quality of a nursing program. A "pass rate" is the percentage of students who underwent the <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/nclex/">NCLEX-RN Exam</a> and successfully passed. This is a public record available on the State's nursing board website. This can be invaluable in determining how well a school is able to educate it's students and help them learn how to pass the NCLEX-RN.</p>
<p>Read <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/guide/considering-rn-school/">what to consider when choosing an RN school</a> and <a href="https://www.registerednursing.org/guide/cost/">how much do RN programs cost</a>.</p>
