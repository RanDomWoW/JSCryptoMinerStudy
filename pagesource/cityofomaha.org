<p>(March 23, 2018)</p>
<p>A nationwide framework for Small and Emerging Business (SEB) opportunities is showing results in Omaha. "REACH", the Midwest’s largest, multi-partner initiative designed to increase certified SEBs, today announced more than $6.1M in contracts realized in the past 30 months – contracts that have increased both access to construction projects and inclusivity.</p>
<p>“Many institutions and businesses, both large and small, have been seeking a way to assist small businesses and give back,” said Winsley Durand, executive director, REACH. “The REACH program has provided a vehicle that provides a collaborative and organized way for these firms to participate in helping to effectively grow our small business ecosystem.”</p>

<p>“Our partnership with the Greater Omaha Chamber’s REACH program has provided new opportunities for small and emerging businesses to grow and compete,” said Omaha Mayor Jean Stothert.&nbsp;&nbsp;“Workforce development is a priority for our administration. 	<h4 class="newsflash-title">
			Improvements Planned for Siena/Francis House		</h4>




<p>(March 22, 2018)</p>
<p>Plans are underway to redevelop the Siena/Francis House campus, improving and expanding services to Omaha’s homeless population.</p>
<p>The Omaha Planning Board will consider an amendment to an existing redevelopment plan for the area between North 16<sup>th</sup> Street and North 18<sup>th</sup> Street, Nicholas Street to the Kellom Greenbelt, which includes the Siena/Francis House campus.&nbsp; The amendment will be on the April agenda.</p>
<p>The plan includes demolition of the current day shelter at 1624 Nicholas, environmental remediation, site preparation and landscaping. The Redevelopment Plan also recognizes the likely future demolition and replacement of the current Siena/Francis House building when it reaches the end of its useful life.&nbsp; The timeline for that phase of the project has not been determined.</p>
<p>&nbsp;“This is an important step to provide services to our citizens who are homeless,” said Mayor Jean Stothert.&nbsp; “Our goal must be to help people transition from homelessness to safe, affordable housing, employment and other services necessary for a successful future.&nbsp; The financing for this project will be possible thanks to Heritage Services. We are grateful for their support.”</p>
<p>&nbsp;“The Heritage Services Board of Directors approved Heritage Services moving forward in good faith to raise $15 million in funding to construct a new low barrier emergency shelter for the Siena/Francis House,” said Heritage Services President Sue Morris.&nbsp; “This new approximately 43,000 square foot facility will lodge roughly 350 single men and 50-100 single women with dignity in a durable and safe space.”</p>
<p>The City will use redevelopment bonds up to $2.5 million for the demolition, site remediation and streetscaping.&nbsp; The funding is currently allocated in the Capital Improvement Plan.</p>

<p>(March 20, 2017)</p>
<p>“The Civic Auditorium site is back on the market,” said Mayor Jean Stothert.</p>
<p>In an email to Mayor Stothert Tuesday, Project 19 LLC Member Zach Wiegert said it would “no longer pursue developing the Civic Site”. &nbsp;Project 19 was the name given to the project by Tetrad Property Group.</p>
<p>“I had faith in Tetrad and I am disappointed that after almost four years, they cannot complete this project,” said Mayor Stothert.&nbsp; “We have a lot of interest and investment in downtown Omaha and someone will develop the Civic site.&nbsp; An interested developer has already contacted me today.”</p>
<p>The City issued the Request for Proposals (RFP) for development of the site in 2014 and selected Tetrad.&nbsp; The company planned a $320 million mixed-use development that included a 355,000 square foot office tower to house a major tenant to be secured by the developer.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Tetrad submitted a preliminary plat for the development in 2017. The Planning Board and the City Council approved it.</p>
<p>In February 2018, Tetrad proposed a new Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) that included more than $40 million in taxpayer contributions in addition to over $50 million in Tax Increment Financing and Enhanced Employment Area Occupation tax incentives.</p>
<p>Tetrad asked for:</p>
<ul>

</ul>
<ul>
<li>Use of the Enhanced Employment Area Occupation Tax to finance maintenance of public areas on the site</li>
</ul>
<ul>

</ul>
<ul>
<li>The City would waive the costs associated with demolition of the auditorium: $3.273 million. Tetrad was originally to reimburse the City for the cost of demolition</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Parking:</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>City to build and pay for a new parking garage estimated to exceed $30 million</li>
</ol><ol start="2">
<li>First rights to lease the existing city-owned garage (Park 5) at no cost to Tetrad for 20 years, which would also displace people who currently lease parking in that garage. The</li>
</ol>
<p>garage is currently 85% occupied</p>
<ol start="3">
<li>Sublease the parking lot under I-480 at the same rate the city leases it from the state</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Commitment of city resources to help secure a major office tenant. In the original proposal, Tetrad agreed to secure the tenant.</li>
</ul>
<p>The City countered this month will an alternate MOU that would be more affordable for the City and the taxpayers.</p>
<p>The City:</p>
<ul>
<li>Agreed to waive the auditorium demolition expenses to assist in the successful redevelopment</li>
</ul>
<p>of the site</p>
<ul>
<li>Required the developer to pay the $20 per square foot for the property, a condition of the original RFP. The City would agree to waive that amount with the successful commitment of a major office tenant to the site</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>TIF financing not to exceed 20% of the total costs of the project</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Agreed to construct and operate the new parking garage (estimated to exceed $30 million) if deemed necessary by the commitment of a major office tenant, offered to the developer at a reduced rate for 5 years</li>
<li>Would maintain ownership and control of the existing parking garage (Park 5) and I-480 lot with spaces leased to the developer at a reduced rate for 5 years</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Would consider use of an Enhanced Employment Area Occupation Tax to support the maintenance of public areas on the site</li>
</ul>
<p>The City’s offer was conditional on the developer successfully securing a major office tenant with over 1,500 employees.</p>
<p>“I am the steward of the taxpayer’s dollars,” said Mayor Stothert. “We work with all developers, but we must be fair. Tetrad was asking for incentives far beyond what we have done for other developers.”</p>
<p>In its letter terminating the agreement, Tetrad also cited delays in the demolition of the Civic Auditorium and obstacles caused by new developments planned for other city-owned property downtown including the Riverfront, Gene Leahy Mall and Lot B, west of the CenturyLink Center.</p>
<p>“I don’t look at all the development downtown as obstacles, I look at it as progress,” said Mayor Stothert.</p>
<p>The City selected Tetrad to develop the Civic site in August 2014.&nbsp; An auction to sell the contents of the Auditorium began in October 2014. In the Spring of 2015, Olssen &amp; Associates was hired to develop the RFP for the demolition contract.&nbsp; The City Council awarded the contract to DeNovo in February 2016.&nbsp; Shortly after, DeNovo declared bankruptcy; the contract was then awarded to Spiritas which agreed to complete the demolition as scheduled.&nbsp; The demolition was completed in July 2017, fifteen months later.</p>
<p>Mayor Stothert said the City will develop a new RFP for a mixed-use development as soon as possible. “All development is market-driven.&nbsp; The landscape downtown has changed a lot since 2014. I feel strongly we will have another developer who will develop this great downtown location.”</p>

<p>Omaha’s next solid waste collection contract is expected to go out for bid by June.</p>
<p>The contract will include collecting garbage, recyclables and yard waste.&nbsp; The current contract with Waste Management expires in 2020. It has been in effect since 2006.</p>
<p>Though still in development, the next contract will require automated trucks and covered, wheeled carts, which is a safer, more efficient system.</p>
<p>“We don’t believe any company will bid if we don’t move to the current industry standard of service,” said Mayor Jean Stothert.</p>
<p>Omaha’s contract is described as one of the largest procurement contracts in the country this year.</p>
<p>The current plan is to deliver three 96-gallon carts to every residential customer, one for garbage, one for yard waste and the third for recyclables.&nbsp; There will be no cost to homeowners. After a 90-day trial, customers can exchange the large cart for a smaller size, most likely 45-48 gallons.&nbsp; Customers may also opt out of one or more carts. For example, if a customer does not generate yard waste, that cart may not be necessary or wanted.</p>
<p>Public surveys conducted over the last year show 9.4 out of 10 households can be served with a 96-gallon cart. (SCS Engineers)</p>
<p>Another significant change in the next contract will be the amount of yard waste that can be left at the curb each week. The current contract with Waste Management allows year-round unlimited yard waste.</p>
<p>“This is not sustainable,” said Mayor Stothert. “There is a significant cost to taxpayers for unlimited yard waste collection, and it’s not sustainable in a long term contract.”</p>
<p>The RFB is expected to require all bidders to include two bids for yard waste collection; one bid for separate collection, a second bid for co-collection with trash. “The cost of yard waste collection will certainly be a factor as we select a new contractor,” said Mayor Stothert. &nbsp;A decision on yard waste collections (separate or co-collection) will be made at a later date.&nbsp; The collection contract does not include the disposal of waste, only the collection processes.</p>
<p>Several new options are being studied to help homeowners dispose of large volumes of yard waste, including a Fall cleanup similar to the Spring Cleanup offered each year, yard waste drop-off sites, and educational programs to reduce yard waste.</p>
<p>The RFB will also require bidders to have a modern fleet of vehicles that use an alternative fuel, likely CNG.</p>
<p>Waste Management currently picks up waste at 145,000 households each week.&nbsp; The current cost is $9.19 per customer, per month, or a total of approximately $16 million annually which is paid from the city’s General Fund. The next contract is expected to cost significantly more, possibly double to an annual cost of $30-$32 million. The 10-year contract will have two five-year renewal options.</p>
<p>Nebraska state statute requires cities of the metropolitan class to provide “basic city services” at no additional cost to taxpayers, which includes solid waste collection. Omaha is the only metropolitan class city in the state.</p>
<p>The City hired SCS Engineers to assist with research, public outreach and development of the RFB. SCS expects there will be multiple bids.&nbsp; The City Council approved the current contract with SCS for approximately $99,000. “This is a huge contract. We need the expertise of a company like SCS to help us develop the best bid possible. SCS is the best company to do that,” said Mayor Stothert.</p>
<p>The RFB is expected to be issued in May or June.&nbsp; There will be a mandatory pre-bid meeting in June or July.&nbsp; Bids will be due in August-September, and a recommendation on a new contractor will likely be made to the City Council by the end of the year. &nbsp;Council members were briefed Tuesday on the status of the RFB development and will have the next 60 days to provide input.</p>
<p>After the Council awards the contract, it will take nine-15 months to phase in the new collection system.</p>
<p>Over the last two years, thousands of citizens have provided input through emails, letters, telephone surveys, a six-month test of an automated system, meetings with community groups, prospective bidders and cart vendors and a series of open houses and demonstrations.&nbsp; That feedback is being considered as the request for bids is developed.</p>
<p>“We have a unique opportunity to modernize the City of Omaha collection system,” said SCS Engineers Vice-President Mike Miller.&nbsp; “We want to get the best contract we can for the citizens of Omaha.”</p>
<p>For more information and resources, go to:&nbsp;&nbsp;http://www.wasteline.org/future.htm</p>

	<h4 class="newsflash-title">
			Track Snow Plows on City Website		</h4>




<p>(February 22, 2018)</p>
<p>A new feature on the city website gives citizens a real-time progress report on snow operations.</p>
<p>“This is an excellent tool to provide up-to-date information about street conditions in the winter,” said Mayor Jean Stothert.</p>
<p>City snow plows are equipped with AVL technology, Automated Vehicle Location. These plows, mainly single and double axle dump trucks that clear main and secondary streets, are marked on the map with white arrows within black circles. The locations update every 60 seconds.</p>
<p>“Today is a great day to demo this because we have an active operation” said Street Maintenance Engineer Austin Rowser.</p>
<p>City crews have been on a 24/7 schedule since early Tuesday morning and will continue at least through the weekend due to additional snow in the forecast.</p>
<p>Users of the new site can see which streets and neighborhoods have been plowed, where plowing is underway and where plowing has not yet started.</p>
<p>“It’s very important to offer a tool that shows where we are and the status of our operation,” said Rowser.</p>
<p>City plows have been equipped with AVL technology for about ten years; it has been used to monitor and manage plowing operations, and investigate citizen complaints and claims.&nbsp; The City contracts with Verizon to provide the expanded technology for the public website. The site is also available on smart phones and tablets.</p>
<p>The City also has 20 companies under contract with approximately 175 additional plows to work in residential areas.&nbsp; These trucks are equipped with GPS but are not yet connected to the new public system.</p>
<p>“We expect this site to evolve,” said Rowser. “Our goal is to have an address lookup so citizens can look up their address and get information about when the plow was on their street.”</p>
<p>The new site is easily accessed on the home page at cityofomaha.org.&nbsp; Click on DOGIS snow operations on the menu.</p>

			Street Resurfacing To Begin in April		</h4>




<p>(February 16, 2018)</p>
<p>Mayor Jean Stothert announced an aggressive 2018 street resurfacing program in the State of the City speech Friday. 58 projects are on the initial schedule.</p>
<p>Funding for these projects comes from multiple sources including the gas tax, vehicle registration fees, general obligation bonds and sewer revenue.</p>
<p>“Citizens tell us they want us to work faster to fix our aging infrastructure, and we’re listening,” said Mayor Stothert. “In just the last five years, we have doubled the street resurfacing budget.”&nbsp;</p>
<p>Contracts have already been awarded for 15 major street projects bid last fall by the State of Nebraska.&nbsp; Those contracts have been awarded and work is scheduled to begin in April.&nbsp; (MAPA<a href="/images/pdf/2018_Resurfacing_3.pdf" target="_blank">&nbsp;5059 (1) </a>) The total cost is $3.9 million.</p>
<p><a href="/images/pdf/2018_Resurfacing_3.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener" title="Click here to download a print quality PDF version of the map."><img src="/images/2018-MAPA-5059-1-Major-Street-Resurfacing-1.png" alt="2018 MAPA 5059 (1) Major Street Resurfacing " /></a></p>
<p>The State of Nebraska will also bid and award contracts for 19 additional major street projects.&nbsp; (MAPA<a href="/images/pdf/2018_Resurfacing_2.pdf" target="_blank">&nbsp;5044</a>(7) ) These projects are budgeted for $5.8 million</p>
<p><a href="/images/pdf/2018_Resurfacing_2.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener" title="Click here to download a print quality PDF version of the map."><img src="/images/2018-MAPA-5044-7-Major-Street-Resurfacing-1.png" alt="2018 MAPA 5044 (7) Major Street Resurfacing" /></a></p>
<p>The City of Omaha will bid the remaining 24 projects, including major and residential streets.&nbsp; The City Council will award the contracts.&nbsp; (2018 Residential/Major)&nbsp; This work is also budgeted for $5.8 million. This work is expected to begin this summer.</p>
<p><a href="/images/pdf/2018_Resurfacing_2.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener" title="Click here to download a print quality PDF version of the map."><img src="/images/2018-Residential-Major-Street-Resurfacing-1.png" alt="2018 Residential Major Street Resurfacing" /></a></p>
<p>All projects are selected based on traffic volume, history of maintenance and related costs, current or upcoming utility work, and the condition of the street using the PASER (Pavement Surface Evaluation and Rating) rating system. &nbsp;PASER is a national average developed by the University of Wisconsin-Madison Transportation Information Center.&nbsp; PASER uses a 1-10 scale to rate the street condition, 1=failed, 10=excellent.&nbsp; The average PASER rating is 7.8 for major, secondary and connector streets in Omaha.&nbsp; Streets with a PASER rating of 4 or lower are prioritized for resurfacing.</p>
<p>In addition to asphalt resurfacing, Mayor Stothert has budgeted $600,000 to continue the brick street repair program.</p>
